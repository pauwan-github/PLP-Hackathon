MPOX Signs & Symptoms

Symptoms usually appear 7-14 days after exposure (but can range from 5 to 21 days) and may last 2-4 weeks. Common signs include:

  • Initial Symptoms (flu-like):
    • Fever
    • Headache
    • Muscle aches and back pain
    • Chills
    • Fatigue
    • Swollen lymph nodes (a key distinguishing symptom from smallpox)
  • Rash:
    • Appears 1-3 days after fever.
    • Progresses through stages: macules → papules → vesicles → pustules → scabs.
    • Often starts on the face and spreads to other parts of the body, including hands, feet, and genital areas.
    • Can be painful or itchy.

Causes & Spread MPOX.

Causes

Mpox is caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the orthopoxvirus genus.
Its natural hosts are wild animals, such as rodents (e.g., Gambian pouched rats) and non-human primates.

How it Spreads

  • Animal-to-human Transmission
    • Consumption of undercooked meat from infected animals.
    • Direct contact with infected animals, their blood, bodily fluids, or lesions.
  • Human-to-Human Transmission:
    • Skin lesions
    • Respiratory droplets (during prolonged face-to-face interaction)
    • Bodily fluids
    • Contaminated clothing, bedding, or surfaces.
    • Sexual contact has been identified as a route of transmission, especially in recent outbreaks.

Prevention & Treatment of MPOX

Prevention Methods

  • Vaccination:
    • The smallpox vaccine offers some protection against mpox.
    • Jynneos (Imvamune/Imvanex): A vaccine approved for mpox prevention.
  • Hygiene Practices:
    • Wash hands regularly with soap and water.
    • Use alcohol-based sanitizers..
    • Avoid touching your face after contact with surfaces or objects in public.
  • Avoid High-Risk Activities:
    • Limit contact with infected individuals and animals.
    • Avoid handling bedding, clothing, or items used by an infected person.
  • Protective Measures:
    • Wear masks and gloves when caring for an infected individual.
    • Use personal protective equipment (PPE) in healthcare settings.

Treatment and Care

  • Supportive Care:
    • Pain relievers (e.g., ibuprofen, acetaminophen).
    • Hydration.
    • Management of secondary infections.
  • Antiviral Medications:
    • Tecovirimat (TPOXX): An antiviral drug approved for treating mpox in severe cases or high-risk individuals.
    • Cidofovir or Brincidofovir may be used in certain cases.
  • Isolation:
    • Infected individuals should isolate until all lesions have scabbed and fallen off.

Note:

  • Early diagnosis and supportive care are vital for better outcomes.
  • Vaccination and preventive measures play a crucial role in controlling the spread.
  • Mpox is not usually fatal, but severe cases can occur, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
